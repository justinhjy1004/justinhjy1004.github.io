Death of a Salesman

20 January 2026


First month of 2026, and I have read quite a few great works. And I can’t quite believe I have finished this book. Death of a Salesman definitely shook me, as I see myself at the start of the journey as what Willy has embarked 34 years ago, to feel the great sense of emptiness that his own subconscious could not hide. The feeling of disappointment, of himself and his unforgiving expectation of Biff, and his final act of sacrifice…

“See, Biff, everybody around me is so false that I’m constantly lowering my ideals…”

I Contain Multitudes

18 January 2026


Ah, all the microbes that surrounds and is part of me. I do remember the transition of much anti-microbial talk, to my mum’s deep interest in shaping my microbiome with probiotics (that worked superb!). It is quite humbling to learn, and have the sense that little microscopic things are pulling the strings to shape our environment, to shape us. And how so many animals can be so fascinatingly weird partly because of how they are, but partly because their microbiomes allow them to be. And how quickly things can turn around, as when we die, they eat us.

Nocturnes: Five Stories of Music and Nightfall

Early January 2026


The most banal of things, turn into a mix of comedy and tragedy at the same time, by the unexplainable choices that people make. And those people are us. We see ourselves at times in these stories (maybe not the Cellist, it was definitely not the best one in the collection). I don’t know who I identify (and fail to identify in these stories, they are so human while not at the same time), but I need to remind myself to never grow into the Krautess of Mavern Hill, even if I have to be Linda in the end of it all.

The Good Earth

4th Jan 2026


It is a little surreal to understand my own parents’ (especially my dad’s) obsession over land. So can be said of my parents’ generation, as I see them thinking first and foremost to own the land beneath our house, and to own other land as a source of wealth.

I see too, the sins of my ancestry (as I like to judge them so quickly) of sexism that plagued the culture I grew up in. The culture I hesitate to call my own, as I see so much of my grandfathers in Wang Lung, inevitably shaped by the expectation, lust, and will of a society so hell bent to prize men over women. The absurd fantasy passing down an eternal lineage. And what is more heritable than the good earth beneath your feet.

The Tyranny of Merit

20th Nov 2025


I remembered when I first finished this book. I talked to it with Sam (Selleck), and we had a long, productive conversation at Chao, at Harvard. Sort of the pinnacle of prestige, the pinnacle of what Sandel is criticizing, as the basis and roots of our poisoned political atmosphere. Hubris, I did feel when I was there, even though I acknowledged it. Humiliation, painfully so when I ‘fell from grace’. Both in many ways, were probably not acts of my own. A lot of it was luck, and I still consider myself an extremely lucky person, to have been graced the many privileges in my life.

But the question that lurks to this day, in living with the bitterness and belief that merit is what you should strive for, to only fall, gives you a different perspective. Rereading this piece of work was important to me. I am glad that someone at work had the same interests as I do, in the folly of trying to be better in this unforgiving world. There is more to being than the constant striving of things. We need to learn that.

Annihilation

20th Nov 2025


Third book that I can recall, which I finished in a day. I don’t even know what to think about it, for the msot part. A very surreal, but real, creeping feeling. But what do I really like about the book? I don’t really know. There is some sort of captivating nature of the biologist, revealing parts of her intentions to explore, what would most think as a suicide mission of sorts. In part discovering the part of her life which she enjoyed a lot, the remote, recluse nature of it all. Perhaps she wouldn’t mind if the whole world was consumed by Area X. Through it all, we still do not know what it is about, truly. Just a surrealscape of things, happening. With wildlife and nature taking over.

But there is another part of it all, what drove her husband to join in such a expedition, to come back a different person. A different person in possibly a very epistemological sense, but the feeling of anger that he left dissipated, for a moment where intimacy (from familiarity) takes over, to have one realize that the crude nature of being is what brings us together. So it was no surprise she could so easily give him up, as it was apparent that he is no longer who he was, to only die soon after. The trail that he left behind, unexpectedly found in the light house, I don’t know what it truly was. As for the Crawler, what the fuck was that? I don’t think we are supposed to truly understand the purpose of it.

Throughout though, I was fascinated by the psychologist. Annihilation!

Night Vision: Seeing Ourselves Through Dark Moods

16th Nov 2025


While the author did not mention ideas from Taoism, especially on the concept of yin and yang, I couldn’t help but relate that to my personal upbringing where that philosophy is more ingrained in me. It is the sense there is always light in darkness, and vice versa. The author’s criticisim of a nyctophobic world, one that cannot tolerate pain, anger, anguish, grief, and anxiety, is a world in which people fail to come to terms with our human nature. And I am often at fault of such behavior, to chastise and dismiss my sadness, grief, and angst; the tingling feeling at the back of my head that is neither crippling nor reassuring, and the constant jitter of the pointlessness of it all. The book came in a rather good and apt time for my psyche, and I am thankful to be reminded that I should not feel shame and regret for the dark feelings I have.

Anxiety: A Philosophical Guide

31st Oct 2025


Desafortunadamente, la gente siempre está, pero no realmente lo está. Es muy fácil perderse en este gran lago de cosas que se llama la vida. Algo con lo que yo (y también mucha gente) tengo que lidiar cotidianamente es la ansiedad. “Sufrimos de ansiedad”, eso es lo que la gente dice cuando hablamos de ella. Es una forma de sufrimiento, ¿pero tiene sentido cuando casi toda la gente tiene que pasar por esto por el simple hecho de ser humano? ¿Puede ser que la ansiedad sea parte del vivir, una mezcla de elecciones y la falta de elecciones, de responsabilidad y la falta de responsabilidad?

No sé realmente qué pienso, pero el autor Samir ha explicado muy bien cómo los filósofos han visto este fenómeno. Desde el punto de vista de que existir es ser ansioso, por nuestra conciencia de que algún día todos moriremos. A todos nos toca este destino. Desde un punto de vista racional, uno puede razonar que esto nos libera para vivir como queramos, aunque es cierto también que tenemos que lidiar con las consecuencias que llegan con nuestros actos. ¿A la vez es cierto que la ansiedad viene de esta gran posibilidad y potencial de hacer cualquier cosa (o joder nuestra vida por completo)?

Mi experiencia con la ansiedad viene también cuando no tengo elecciones por la situación en que estoy. No fue la libertad de elegir, sino la falta de ella lo que me causó mucha angustia. Y, a la vez, es el hecho de que tenemos esta “responsabilidad” de vivir sin arrepentirse y la realización de que nuestro tiempo aquí en el universo es solo una pequeña, insignificante parte de su timeline lo que me da esta sensación de incomodidad. Claro, por eso, es algo tan difícil de definir y entender, algo que casi todos nosotros podemos identificar inmediatamente, cuando esta angustia irrumpe en nuestra mente sin aviso.

Kafka on the Shore

20 Oct 2025


I don’t think anyone knows what Kafka on the Shore is truly about, and I mean even Murukami himself would have a hard time explaining in its entirety of this Oedipal story. There is something so interesting about how he writes though, drawing me in as the parallel stories unfold. I think it is hard to disappoint with talking to cats, though it is weird that he wrote about a middle-aged woman having sex with a fifteen year old boy.

But at least I see this as an allegory of some sort (I am not sure if allegory is the correct word), one about the past (Miss Saeki), one ‘trapped’ in the constant present (Nakata), and the one running from his future (Kafka Tamura). Whose love holds her back and sucks her soul, leaving herself hollow. With another withstanding the cruel world he was tossed into, yet undeniably enjoyed and lived the best life amongst all the characters, and the last who so desire to flee his future to only fulfill the prophecy made of him. All, whose paths change due to unforeseen circumstances, whose circumstances change their paths too.

Privacy is Hard and Seven Other Myths

Mid Oct 2025


Hoepman (the author) failed to convince me that privacy is not hard! I think it is more accurate to say that Privacy is not as Hard as you think, though I think that will not sell as well. I did pick up this book primarily because of the very provocative title that purportedly dispel the myth that privacy is difficult. I thoroughly enjoyed the book, describing intuitively the various mechanisms (from cryptographic, to VPNs, to the dangers and prevalance of metadata and their associated harms, and general tracability of the things you do online, and zero-knowledge proofs! Crazy stuff) that can help us live a more private online life. I am glad that he touches many aspects of it, describing the choices we make in terms of design and purpose that can help us be more private, but I would not certainly say that privacy is not hard.

Note: I never expected finding the best description of public key encryption in this book. I thought the analogy of private communication between Elizabeth and Mr. Darcy was great, with the public key being the box with a lock that Mr. Darcy gives to Elizabeth, and the private key is his own when Elizabeth returns the box (locked) containing her message!

The Ladies’ Paradise

25 Sep 2025


The beautiful scent of budding capitalism at the end of 19th Century Paris. Women flocking to their paradise, as Mouret constructed his ultimate fort to conquer them, with endless things they can wear, and even more things to buy. But this is not only the story of the victors of the days. No, it is way more than that as Zola brings us to life around, inside, and under the weight of the Paradise Mouret built.

A story of love, of purity, but also as a constant observer of the destruction that can be brought upon people on the periphery. I was most struck by the story of poor Genevieve, who decays and declines as her sense (even though with the knowledge) that her promised husband, Colomban, will never come to be. The pain that the Baudu family have to go through, though there is little that one can blame on someone else for their misfortunes, rings true to this day. The machine goes on, now called ‘creative destruction’ (by economist) wrecks havoc to many, while fortune and fame rise to take their place.

The Unbearable Lightness of Being

24 Sep 2025


I read the edition with the sad dog cover, and as I was reading the first two-thirds (perhaps) even more of the book, I was wondering, “why the sad dog?”. There is just so often this jarring feeling, the choices these people make, the choices that they have to contend with. And this universal feeling and question of whether we have made the right decision(s) in life will always haunt us. But we very well should know, understand, and perhaps most importantly accept the fact that down this personal path of yours, every step is a different step, and though they may sound the same, the chaos in life will always lead you down a diverging path.

Einmal ist keinmal, that rings both so loudly yet hollowly in my ears. Almost a paradox, where 1 is clearly not 0 (in most cases), but true in the sense that in the grand scheme of things, a speck could very well be nothing. To be fair, anything how big, with sufficient distance and over time, would slowly disappear. Lost in time, in ways that we can never recover. And what a wonderful fact that is, to know that your one short life may very well be pointless after all. Those absurd missions, the must haves, the duties that were bestowed upon us, they don’t really matter (in the long run). But as we take each step, it feels like we have to take the right one. We are constantly optimizing something, making sure that we are on the right track, and that fear that things might go wrong is oh so unbearable. But we know that they are absurd and pointless at the end of the day. So why torture ourselves for it? All we do, it may as well not have happened. But it matters to you now, Justin. So do what matters.

The Right to Oblivion: Privacy and the Good Life

12 Sep 2025


I’ve always wrestled with a personal paradox of mine, the fact that I think privacy is an extremely important good, and something that I would want to have, as a birthright of sorts, yet I find myself relenting to the internet, or more specifically, the tools we use to access the internet as we know it today, in terms of the data that they want, freely for them to use. I think I am not the only one that look at how I use the internet and think of that Widerspruch.

I think however, this book has provided me a realization that, what I want is indeed privacy and do value it. What I don’t want however, is the constant need to hide, hide my information from data brokers that use information. Hide from the constant scrutiny not from people (or individuals) per se, but from the peering eyes of Big Tech of using my data for commercial means. It is weird that so much of me is on the internet, as I am writing this and publishing this in the internet. But I’d feel almost even more violated privacy wise if a rando so happen to find this page of mine, and to learn of the things that I know, before really knowing me as a person. I don’t think I am profound, and so any profundity that I do have, I’d like to keep. A little mystery. A little part of me that’s just lived, and never relived.

Around the World in 80 Trains

Sometime Aug 2025


Reading this memoir/biography was a little meta to me. For one, I am traveling with Neinei (and my family 2 weeks prior) around the world (Europe and SAE, so not exactly) for a large part by train. Of course, inevitably, more so was done in airplanes but I do not have the time, freedom, patience, nor the ability to sit through the trans-Siberian rail to Europe. By Rajesh’saccount, I do not want to either, as long as I intend to live as according to Russia, Malaysians are a type that ‘is attracted to crime’.

Fantastic book, for a fantastic time learning about trains, and a few of them I have taken while on this trip that were also described and mentioned in this book! That was definitely a fun experience to have, and a semi-guide for the unexperienced traveler who had his first time on an overnight train. I wouldn’t say I am a big fan, but no longer am I afraid of it. Sounds picky, and definitely I am, Rajesh mentioned something that irked me, though I believe that I once held that opinion. I agree that picture obsession is a weird culture. Instagrammable is the most hated word I know from the English dictionary, and Rajesh snidely commented how people think that imposing their face on a well-known place/figure/statue or whatever you may see in a trip somehow imbues that image with some sort of great qualities to you, when thousands if not millions of people had done the same. I used to hold that opinion, and relegated the cameras to the side. Now I wish I have taken more pictures. They help me relive memories that otherwise have hidden deep in my unconsciousness. I miss you baby, I miss you a lot.

Great Expectations

30 June 2025


A book perfect for my current situation, as I sit here in Malaysia, feeling crestfallen after my unsuccessful bid to reach the American dream, and my parting with my American boy, from an American city that I love, and will probably love for a long, long time. I had no Great Expectation before my journey in Boston, a tumultous time of just many horrible things, but I’ve learned to love a great city, and a greater squoose.

And that’s Pip! From his humble beginnings, up to the echelons of British Victorian society, to ‘fall’ from grace, but in the arms of people who love, who truly are worth the time, and to leave behind whatever prestige the university name could ever provide me. I know a part of me still thinks that it matters, but we all know that that is not true. At the top of the crust, la mierda sale aflote. I took all in its looks, basking in its fame, but I should have taken everything on evidence. There is no better rule.

Surprised, quite honestly, as I thought this is a Victorian novel that would praise Victorian values, aesthetics, and sensibilities. A romatic novel of a young boy escaping poverty, and learning about the niceties that the gentle, London society could offer in the Victorian era; to so quickly flip it at the last chapter of the second part was so brilliant of Dickens’ part, and to realize then, and to constantly reflect, the same flaws, prejudice, and superiority I have, and I see around constantly. And why such condescension to normal people when I see how ungentle the gentle society can be?

From Boom to Bubble: How Finance Built the New Chicago

Sometime in April 2025


Interesting book documenting how the financial cycle can in so many ways spill into the construction cycle, not only as a leading indicator as most economist would assume (ie. As demand for new buildings increase, which then leads to construction firms requiring more loan, and hence the financial system from that way constrains or expands construction booms and bust), but as an active player in pushing construction to happen or not. The author provides rather good evidences and narration as to how finance (or the availability of liquidity from the financial markets) pushes construction to be done, instead of the other way around that most people assume.

All the Lights We Cannot See

Soon after New Year 2025


It’s difficult to do the right thing when times are easy, more so when it is not. Doerr manages to capture this feeling, and struggle, with the backdrop of World War II, the pain and the active choices one makes in demanding circumstances. I think Doerr on top of that, captures with his narrative structure of going back and forth between Werner’s and Marie-Laure’s lives, the serendipitous nature of life, and of course, the stories were eventually woven together. Though through it all, we craft our own stories, our own legends, be it true or otherwise, in our own minds, compacted like useless carbon.

everthing I know about love

26th December 2024


I’d love to think of myself of a very unique person, with a very special story that no one else has ever experienced. That is true in the most technical, pedantic sense, and our society does reinforce that concept, again and again, that your value comes from the ability to distinguish. Yet books like Alderton’s make me realize how similar our lived experiences are, as I mapped her dream-hazed teenage years, her wild party girl years, the unadulterated exposé of early adulting, and just the mess our 20s can be. Being solidly in my mid-twenties, I cannot help but laugh and ache at the things we have to go through.
